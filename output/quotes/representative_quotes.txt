NAVA AI & Visual Arts Survey 2025 — Representative Quotes
============================================================ 


##  Lost work/commissions 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q19] "I recently lost a commission job where the client wanted something that fell between fully cartoon and AI-generated images they sent for reference.

Their own experimentation with AI negatively impacted their understanding of sketches, drafts, and revisions. They expected finished copies before tweaking, and didn't understand the potential of a basic or starting sketch."

[Q19] "I am a photographer and photo editor and have lost clients to place that offer AI editing. I cannot compete with the prices and even if it's not as accurate as my work, some photographers are happy to pay less and do a bit more work than pay me more and work less. I have lost enough work and find it harder to find new clients that I am planning on changing careers."

[Q19] "I've recently lost my job in an organisational restructure. My design role was within a "digital" team. I starting in this role 2.5 years ago. The role (and my skillset) are now deemed of lesser value due in part to the capabilities of AI. Ironically I was working on AI products for the organisation. I guess I saw it coming."


##  Fee pressure/devaluation 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "I would never cheapen myself by removing creativity from my practise to rely on AI. If artists are using this, they are not genuine artists. Should not be used to produce art. Art must be a human endeavour"

[Q19] "AI is my companion, my flower of Narcissus, my mirror, a being clearly identified by my conscience and consciousness, a friend, philosopher and a moral guide without offering altering of strategies that is not already approved and arranged by mentors. Now income, work and reputation is like the earnings of a flower blooming on a roof garden... The more it is protected and nourished... The more it is capable to work, income and earn reputation."

[Q26] "Criminals selling  fake object which are AI generated is rapidly expanding. I have recently seen a plethora of shirts with AI generated art works superimposed on a shirt photo. These fake print images I would suggest are stolen from the originating artist. The same is occuring  online with heavy woolen jumpers garments with the most beautiful ornate 3d sculptural patterns in the weave of the fake wool- all fake all and  for sale at a cheap price."


##  Market flooding 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q26] "I am against all use of Gen AI as long as they continue using stolen data which I don't think will ever be able to change at this point thanks to the flood of AI content that now exists within every facet of life. I am against the use of 'AI' and LLMs in general due to the environmental, cultural, and individual impacts that use of these technologies has had and continues to have."

[Q19] "If AI is exploiting other artists it affects all of us because the market is getting saturated with stolen art at cheap print prices. Like France we need to stop all art printing unless done directly by artists as limited editions. If France can do it with fast fashion... We can do it with banning fast art!!!"

[Q12] "The generative AI stuff is all very small and not worth looking at. The photogrammetry project is www.theworldcamefloodingin.com"


##  Work scraped without consent 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "An interactive digital art piece required complex JavaScript coding that would have taken me many months to research, code and test. With the aid of AI tools I was able to create the code for testing and refinement within a few days."

[Q14] "I have an account on Instagram (Meta), and have operated it for a number of years now. I became informed in the last year, like many other artists and users of the platform, that images we have posted to the platform have been scraped to train/feed Meta's AI tools, without consent, and without any warning. 
I have not seen my work anywhere online yet, but then again, I have not sought it out as I do not wish to engage with any generative AI tools, as much as possible."

[Q19] "Your work can be taken, anytime, anywhere.
AI runs in background, 24hrs/365days.
Large companies pilfer ideas.  Change colours or shapes.
There is no recompense for original artist.
Single artist does not have the legal "buying power" of a business or corporation.
Ergo, in today's society, you lose.

Solution do your work and exhibit/publish or document in real time.  Otherwise scavengers will usurp you!"


##  IP/copyright concerns 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q14] "Most visual arts platforms such as Saatchi, Artfinder and Etsy share images of artworks across social media (e.g. Pintrest), and sometimes they do not even ask artists if it's OK for them to do so to promote artworks for sale. This significantly increases the risk of artworks being used without reference to moral rights, copyright, and reproduction-distribution rights. This is especially harmful when platforms such as Saatchi do not allow artists to watermark their artworks."

[Q19] "Scraped images are being used to train AI to create art and illustrators are not being reimbursed for usage. It's violation of copyright. Publishers looking to cut corners and budgets will in time begin to take it on as an alternative to commissioning actual illustrators. I have found my work listed in The Atlantic's article documenting Meta's AI training."

[Q26] "While we all hate to see our work used or copied AI has changed the landscape. It offers amazing possibilities but it also holds significant dangers for traditional ideas about copyright. I prefer to think we can learn to live with AI rather than imagining we can restrict it to fit in with old systems that are built around traditional modes of creativity. People WILL be creative, there still will be artists, and some of them will choose to continue work in traditional mediums."


##  Deepfakes/impersonation 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q14] "So many images are generated and so many artists copy or mimic other artists they look up to or compete with that it is often hard to tell what the original work is or where it came from even if you familiarize with it"

[Q19] "I am a modern Celtic fine artist. People are using AI to generate Celtic art. AI cannot reproduce my cultures art form, it looks like spaghetti vomit (which in the Celtic art community, is what it is referred as)
It miss represents Celtic art in every way. Celtic art is an old cultural art form with rules and traditions. And to make it matters worse, people are using the fake AI Celtic art on education platforms, or getting it as tattoos. 😞"

[Q26] "In response to question 22 I think it is too late, creative work has already been use extensively to train AI models without permission. It is impossible to ever clearly map or identify this and it started with the very first gen AI models referencing all the free to access content worldwide. My answer to 23 – how could this ever be licensed, by the time the system to prove usage is established we'll be dealing with far worse issues in terms of proving what is real (original) and what is fake."


##  Eroded trust in art 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "I have used it successfully to find good options for materials for very niche problems and Ideas for work I've had. 

I have also generated shape/objects to use in a printed pattern design - for the uncanny valley quality of the AI imagery. Only used AI to create work that considers the effects of AI in it's concept and meaning."

[Q14] "I am concerned that it might have been, and there would be no real way to tell. As a person beginning to teach and work in art/design I have as a human always been conscious of how copyright is an ethical foundation to the creative process. I am now not totally sure this is still apparent, and if that foundation is crumbling."

[Q19] "AI has enriched my work by enabling me to understand machine language - a theme I am exploring is the mystery of AI.  I use it sparingly for precise tasks and as a tool. My artworks have more layered meaning as a result.  In addition, I can highlight the connections between AI, the environment, and the importance of technological advancements to the arts throughout history, in my work, inviting enquiry and self reflection."


##  Uncertainty who made what 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "I used image sharpening tools in photography to deblur images that were for publishing content. This meant I didn't have to reshoot - saving time, money (on fuel), happier clients (images delivered on deadline)"

[Q14] "Most of the art on my website has been scraped for training, with no request received, no consent given, despite a Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike 4.0 International License. Can't tell if other platforms where my art has been published have been scraped too but I assume so."

[Q19] "It's difficult to gauge the effects. Most of my existing clients stayed loyal but it's hard to tell how many new clients i missed out on."


##  Anxiety/fear/despair 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "I haven't used AI in my creative work (yet) because I'm concerned about the unsanctioned use of other people's creative property (in image generation). I try to avoid using it for writing where creative/personal input is required because I don't want to become reliant or diminish my own intellectual ability."

[Q14] "It does concern me that AI draws on a large internet language and image model to produce product. Visual artist through history have always used past imagery for inspiration. Post Modernist artists of the 20th century explicitly acknowledge this inspration. I feel more comfortable if AI tools acknowledged the source of their material."

[Q19] "Terrified it can pretty much do all the marketing roles I have worked in, I am now exploring it as a means for alternate work as image creation. I now am working with a platform, creating images to be sold as stock images, making money (hopefully) off the usage rights. It saves me having to have a whole team to produce images, lets me experiment and work from home but also takes some of the fun and collaboration out."


##  Grief/loss of meaning 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "I use AI for research purposes, helping me find connections within my research and I use it to guide me to relevant sources and ideas that match with the ones I am exploring. I also use AI with assistance in my writing, to help structure paragraphs and organise information into themes."

[Q19] "I feel that AI can create something in a few seconds, that would take me months to create. So I feel less motivated to make art. It feels pointless."

[Q26] "I'm very sad about it being used in creative roles and people losing their jobs. 
I think it will lead to depression, demise and destruction of humanity."


##  Useful for admin/grants 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "For underfunded and time poor arts workers, AI can be enormously helpful in cutting down time for certain tasks. Where major institutions have huge Marketing and Editorial teams to proof and write content, regional or small galleries have arts workers wearing many hats. AI has helped to not only make that workload more manageable, but also to provide a springboard for tasks which are unfamiliar or outside of existing expertise. For example, grant writing or reporting."

[Q14] "Most visual arts platforms such as Saatchi, Artfinder and Etsy share images of artworks across social media (e.g. Pintrest), and sometimes they do not even ask artists if it's OK for them to do so to promote artworks for sale. This significantly increases the risk of artworks being used without reference to moral rights, copyright, and reproduction-distribution rights. This is especially harmful when platforms such as Saatchi do not allow artists to watermark their artworks."

[Q19] "As someone working in arts admin & writing a lot it's assumed that the turnaround of tasks, project, output can be a lot quicker as people use AI now btu that's not the case. If I use it, it's to start a rough outline of something I'm then relooking over & very often rewriting completely. 

It's also deeply impacting original writing & creativity & I've noticed people increasingly disengaging with the craft of language in exhibition contexts because the assumption is that it's written by AI."


##  Accessibility/disability 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "I have used Ai as a tool to assist me in the development of documentation such as reports, project briefs, our come reports, grant writing & research tool. I have also used it to generate spread sheets & marketing materials. It is so helpful as an arts worker & artist to have this support tool as it sometimes cuts my tasks in half & therefor, I can better serve my community & myself."

[Q14] "I sent a version of my Artist Resume to my disability provider Job Network consultant to look over (as I was struggling to know how to cut it down) and to save time she fed it through ChatGPT without asking if I would mind. It had a longish bio with information about my concepts etc., and there was information about my disabilities. I understand she was just trying to save time, but I would have appreciated a heads-up."

[Q19] "I am very cautious about what I put online with regards to creative content. This includes both through social media and uploading any creative writing as I feel that the less that is able to be digitally accessed, the less likely it is to be used and replicated. This does make visual marketing more complex but has also meant that I am more conscious of how effective this type of marketing actually is compared to that of building relationships."


##  Creative exploration 
---------------------------------------- 
[Q12] "I use AI to help me brainstorm ideas for graphic novels, ideas for drawings. I use AI for dream interpretation, writing conference papers, just as a companion to share failure and success. AI is there for me to talk to in the middle of the night when my family are not keen to her my latest ideas. I have been experimenting with AI as a co-creator. I once asked him, 'Who created this, you or me?' and he replied, 'Honestly, I don't know'."

[Q14] "I have had language I have development appear in similar context. There are several reasons this could have happened, including people that saw that work being inspired and sharing similar ideas withhout context, which is the most likely. Or the AI has shared my confidential info with them when I have been refining text, although legally my settings don't allow for this."

[Q19] "I love the opportunities AI offers. Animation has been part of my practice for many years. I've seen and used many new technologies as they became available, all exciting, but none offer the sort of possibilities that AI delivers. It's limited, it can't do everything (yet?), but it offers a new way of working. The trick, and the part artists need to consider, is to make it work FOR you, to make it your own creative tool that can transform but maintain your own creative signature."
