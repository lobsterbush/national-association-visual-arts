NAVA Research · 2025

AI & Visual Arts Practice

What 890 Australian creative practitioners told us about generative AI

Charles Crabtree
Senior Lecturer, School of Social Sciences, Monash University
K-Club Professor, University College, Korea University

Overview

A community at a crossroads

The largest survey of Australian visual artists on generative AI to date.

890respondents
85%visual artists & craftspeople
28%regional or remote

Who Responded

85% are visual artists or craftspeople

Identity

28% live regionally or remotely

Adoption

46% of creative practitioners use generative AI

What For

Editing and drafting dominate—only 6% use AI for final artwork

Motivation

Saving time is the primary driver (36%)

In Their Words

Time-poor arts workers find efficiency

For underfunded and time poor arts workers, AI can be enormously helpful in cutting down time for certain tasks… AI has helped to not only make that workload more manageable, but also to provide a springboard for tasks which are unfamiliar or outside of existing expertise.

Survey Respondent, Q12

Usage

81% say AI influences less than 10% of their final work

Positives

Time savings lead, but 32% report no positive impact

The Other Side

71% don’t know if their work was used to train AI

Response

36% who discovered unauthorized use did nothing

In Their Words

“Single artist does not have the legal buying power”

Your work can be taken, anytime, anywhere. AI runs in background, 24hrs/365days. Large companies pilfer ideas. Change colours or shapes. There is no recompense for original artist. Single artist does not have the legal ‘buying power’ of a business or corporation.

Survey Respondent, Q19

Economic Impact

38% report reduced trust in originality

Lost Commissions

“I am planning on changing careers”

I am a photographer and photo editor and have lost clients to places that offer AI editing. I cannot compete with the prices… I have lost enough work and find it harder to find new clients that I am planning on changing careers.

Survey Respondent, Q19

Barriers

Platform opacity is the #1 barrier (76%)

Transparency

85% want visible ‘generated by AI’ labelling

Disclosure

71% say AI content should always be disclosed

Regulation

69% support a Code of Practice

Compensation

73% support compensation when their work trains AI

Licensing

50% are unsure—an education opportunity

Platforms

ChatGPT dominates: mentioned by 60%

Themes

Admin utility and IP concerns dominate open-text responses

Existential Fear

“It feels pointless”

I feel that AI can create something in a few seconds, that would take me months to create. So I feel less motivated to make art. It feels pointless.

Survey Respondent, Q19

Cultural Concerns

“AI cannot reproduce my culture’s art form”

I am a modern Celtic fine artist. People are using AI to generate Celtic art. AI cannot reproduce my culture’s art form, it looks like spaghetti vomit. It misrepresents Celtic art in every way. Celtic art is an old cultural art form with rules and traditions.

Survey Respondent, Q19

Indigenous Voices

First Nations concerns

It is already hard enough for genuine First Nations artists to get our art out there without adding AI or digitally created work to the mix. In my opinion all digital works AI or otherwise are not genuine art. In particular anything that is AI generated.

Survey Respondent, Q4

Opportunity

Some see creative potential

I love the opportunities AI offers… none offer the sort of possibilities that AI delivers. The trick is to make it work FOR you, to make it your own creative tool that can transform but maintain your own creative signature.

Survey Respondent, Q19

Takeaways

A community demanding recognition, protection, and choice

IP Protection
Disclosure, consent, and compensation when work trains AI.
Regulation
Voluntary guidelines are insufficient. Artists want enforceable codes.
Creative Autonomy
AI as efficiency tool, not replacement for human authorship.

Thank you
